biography

my practice aims to investigate transformation processes and facilitate spaces for collective imaginings of presents and futures. With a background in Sociology and Architectural Design, I am passionate about understanding how social structures are embedded in ever-changing global environments and how we can collectively react to ongoing (climate) emergencies.
I believe that exchange and discussion are key to opening up new possibilities for seeing and thinking, and promoting heterogeneous coexistences. I am committed to creating social spaces and pushing the boundaries of what's possible.

more thAn I

see more

“more thAn I” is a project aimed at creating experiences that encourage people to engage with more-than-human intelligences in a collaborative way. The installation investigates potentialities of diversifying narratives (about presents, pasts and futures) by initiating a collective storytelling that transcends into a ‘more-than-human’ space.

it is a collaborative storytelling in which an AI- Language Model asks questions while paticipants watch their answers being weaved into a continuous story and projected in real time. Their input merges with the one from the participant beforer and the ones that come after, forming a fascinating plot.

the aim is to create a space for critical engagement with AI-tools and the (social) structures that underlie human-machine interactions and outcomes. What happens if we mix a ‘human collective’ and an ‘artificial’ intelligence? Is it an actual ‘mix’?

watersituationships: an autoethnographic research

this is an investigation of the availability and quality of water in Barcelona through experimenting with the first-person perspective method of autoethnography. By living on publicly accessible water for 24 hours without causing the flow of water myself, I tried to become aware of who I share this water with. I try to live as a non-human inhabitant of the city to investigate who has access to fresh water.

my observations show how difficult it is to deviate from an anthropocentric perspective in an environment that is primarily meant for humans. Nevertheless, it raises questions about my (human) average daily water consumption (112 l), the treatment of water for human consumption and the right to define what is “healthy” water and who is allowed to have access to it. For example, what comes out of water treatment plants after being filtered and treated could be considered dead or even poisoned from a nature perspective.

I documented my 24-hour challenge in a one-minute video.

speculative narration: facilitation of storytelling workshops for transitioning

along three overarching concepts related to transitioning: unfamiliarise, grief, and rebirth, the goal of these workshop-series is to facilitate space for imagining alternative realities. Participants are encouraged to engage with unfamiliar positions, process feelings of grief, and explore ideas for new beginnings. We aim to go beyond our individual selves but rather engage with more than us (the facilitators Myrto and me), (and maybe even ‘more-than-human’ actants) to inspire to new ways of thinking and being in the world.

one of the topics we chose for a workshop was “Transition in the Educational System: From In-person to Hybrid Forms of Learning and Teaching.” Here we explore the challenges and opportunities presented by this transition, and imagine alternative ways of approaching education in the future.

the output of these workshops is the input for an experimentation with Artificial Intelligence creativity and the merging between human and more-than- human imagination. We fed parts of the outcome into Language models and Visualisation models.

social mosaic: an exploration of the social fabric that connects us all

this project draws inspiration from Anna Tsing’s anthropological writing on the (social) connective potential of Matsutake mushrooms. The project involved the creation of 500 clay figures, each resembling a small mushroom. I give these figures to friends and family, to people I stay with or meet in a train and have a good connection with.

the concept of mycelium, the underground network of fungus that connects trees and plants in a forest, inspired the idea of creating invisible connectors through the distribution of these clay figures. I aim to emphasise the potential for connection and collaboration that exists within unconscious (social) networks: somehow, we are all interconnected.

UP2US

see more

this project was developed as part of the Circular Cities Challenge by the Distributed Design Platform (DDP) and FabLab Barcelona. Our team of four worked to find an answer to the challenge faced by the energy sector in its attempt to transition to renewable energy sources: How to deal with the time mismatch between energy consumption and energy production?

if energy from renewable sources is high during sunny or windy hours and no one uses it, we leave it unused (waste it?), while if consumption exceeds energy production from renewable sources at night, when the sun is no longer shining and the wind has calmed down, we make up the deficit with energy from non-renewable sources and batteries. Therefore, to fully transition to renewable energy sources, an awareness of the availability and physicality of energy is required.

UP2US aims to address the problem of unused hours at a community level. An installation in public space makes people aware of the energy mismatch. Corresponding colours and an accompanying digital interface translate data about the current availability of energy. Communities can independently adjust their behaviour to reduce pressure on the energy grid during peak hours and work towards a more balanced energy system that can be supported by renewable energy sources.

the traveller

a joint investigation on the changing and complex systems we inhabit, the power dynamics with our fellow critters, and the potential of imagining fictional scenarios, lead the will to engage in dialogue with a more unspecified public. To extend our discussions beyond our social circles, my friend and collegue Myrto-Eirini Pappa and I created an online platform and a traveling sculpture (that resides in cafes for some time) with a QR code leading to the virtual space.

we believe that transition is a vital aspect of structuer we are livig in and have identified three common stages: unfamiliarize, grief, and rebirth. By letting go of norms and values, experiencing distress and suffering, and ultimately being liberated by the past, new entities can emerge and grow. The blue and green cards on the sculpture carry (provocative) questions that hopefully inspire people to interact with us.

with this project we hope to diversify the narratives of the future through a commulative and collective intelligence.

MOC (museum of colonialization)

see more

the Museum of Colonization (MOC) is a project aimed at creating initiatives that encourage people to envision decolonial futures.

we are a group of multidisciplinary designers that came together with the goal of developing interventions that focus on non-western- centric futures. The project is based on the thought that the whole world is a museum of colonialization, you just don’t see it. We have created artifacts and prototypes that challenge colonialism and capitalistic structures while promoting the recognition of colonization and its remaining consequence in our societies.

the museum started as an interactive machine that works just like a gumball dispenser. Visitors can click the “YES” button, as in “I agree to visit the museum”, to receive a ball that contains a sticker with a “fun fact” about a colonial criminal who has been celebrated in some way around Barcelona.

we then created a stamp-button that leaves an imprint on any chosen surface when pressed. As the user pushes the button, a speaker is activated, declaring “That’s so colonial”, this draws attention to the guerrilla act being performed. Meanwhile, the user’s GPS location is automatically added to an online MOC-Map. The image stamped is a QR code that directly links to that map, where users can see their pinned location and those of all previous users.

the most recent project is a “free walking tour” in Barcelona that aims to foster caring dialogues around the colonial history of European cities and connect it to our present realities. we aim to provide context and background by bringing attention to the impacts of (current) colonialist structures and its impacts. With that we hope to make visitors question their assumptions.

safe places

see more

in 2016, I went to Ecuador to help build a school in Aiguas Frias in the Atacames region, which is very affected by earthquakes and had recently experienced the effects of a 7.9 magnitude quake. I stayed with a host family who took care of a large wooden house. The family consists of eight people of almost all ages. They experienced the tremors and soon after they became afraid of what it means to live far from the ground. They moved into the chicken shed, which was no bigger than a few square metres. I slept in the big house alone. The white European privileged girl could not to squeeze in with the eight people and their chickens on the dirty floor. I felt scared there on my own.

in this sequence of images, models, collages and animations, I am trying to reconstruct and simulate the feeling the tremors must have left them with. I use my own memories of staying there, the images I have from that time and place and related footage I get from open-source.

reimagining the campus: a place for collaboration and pausing

see more

the outdoor-campus of the Gerrit Rietveld Academie for Arts and Crafts is a vibrant space where students and faculty members come together. In its middle stands a pavilion (also designed by Gerrit Rietveld), which was originally intended to serve as a sculpting workshop. However, over the years, it has been repurposed as a student-run exhibition place. Despite its central location, the pavilion lacks adequate infrastructure to facilitate interaction and relaxation.

in order to identify the needs and opportunities for improvement, I conducted an ethnographic study of the pavilion on campus. This research revealed that while the pavilion is successfully used as an exhibition space, the campus generally lacks infrastructures and places for meeting and sharing. Students and teachers find it difficult to come together and find inspiration in exchange, which results in a disjointed and transient atmosphere in the yard of the academy.

the aim of this project is to propose a redesign for the infrastructure of the pavilion to increase its visibility and attraction as well as to create a space that encourages collaboration and pausing. My proposal maintains the exhibition space as it is while introducing new elements inspired by Gerrit Rietveld’s vision of interaction and co-creation.

space speaks

based on Jacques Tati’s movie Mon Oncle, this maquette tries to capture the scenery of the set, keeping out its actors and motions. The model is a 1:500 model of the actual house (as I imagine it and drew it). The model emphasise the flagstones that pave a path in the garden. These are representative of more peculiar designs of spaces shown in the movie that propose very specific ways of moving in it. On the property, it seems that only real life (in the model a big plant that grows where it wants) can disturb the structured order.

in the photographs, dramatic lighting and catching angles make the scenery show the tension the movie’s narrative, a young boy (Gérard), experiences. He is living with his parents in a modernist house whose order and functionality constrain him in the playful nature of his childhood.

50/50 refuge

here I investigate the concept of borders and their impact on society.

in 2021, on 10 May, a military strike kills seven civilians. In the aftermath, another 119 people are killed over a dispute over land, and the right to walk on the streets. Shocked by the unpredictability and spontaneity of these events, I am questioning the made-up lines governments draw on ground and that cause fear and death in so many spaces. This cube floating in the air, blowing with the wind wherever it blows, imagines a safe space where there is no room for fear of soil-related consequences.

I explore the relationship between solids and air, emphasizing that there can be no solids without spaces to fill and no visibility of air and space without solids to give them scale. The piece invites the viewer to consider the role of air as a refuge, as the lighter and potentially more neutral element in contrast to the heavy and divisive nature of frontiers drawn on the ground.

dutch design week - home

the breath in a vacuum

in this installation, I explore the complex and intimate relationship between the concept of home and the essential elements required for human and non-human life - air, soil and water. At rest and serenity, the human body requires about half a litre of air, which is equivalent to one complete inhalation. To breathe for one minute, a human takes an average of twelve breaths. For this reason, the work displays twelve net-like vessels that represent air. Each has a volume of half a litre, thus a total of one minute of life. The natural mineral metal lying on the floor symbolises the soil, and the reflection that the shaped surface of the metal creates on the ceiling represents water.

this work reflects, represents and redesigns the dynamic and intimate relationships that enfold our homes. I want to go beyond the personal and encourage finding trust in what physically holds life, what nurtures life, what is home to us all. When we trust, we care. When we care, we sustain.

“The Breath in a Vacuum” was exhibited at Dutch Design Week 2021 as part of the group exhibition “A House is Not, a Home is Not, a House, a Home...” at the Klokgebouw in Eindhoven in October 2021.

part of this research is an audio piece titles: “If you would live here, you would be home by now” what do we mean by being at home? In many languages there is a distinction between a house and a home - even though (in the societies I grew up in during my upbringing) homes are mostly places in houses. So, what is it that fills the space? What gives emotional value to the built structure?

paper aura

to become as tall as the tallest person on earth (2019: 251 cm), I made a costume entirely out of newspaper and glue. The costume creates a wing-like appearance, so you don’t just measure 251 cm in height, but also in width; it gives you a radius of 251 cm.

with this project, I explore the concept of physical presence and the space that people occupy.

people take up space - some more, some less. Never is the space a person occupies just their height, rather the mere presence of people has an impact on their immediate surroundings through their mental emanation.

ghost machine

the project involved repurposing materials from two old computers and designing something new. The screens, speakers, and LEDs are integrated with an Arduino to create a machine that can detect the presence of people (sensitive to change in light intensity) and respond with sound and light.

the result is an installation in which a storytelling blurs the lines between the seen and unseen. In the story, the flowers provide a way to engage with the hidden energies in our surroundings, to ghosts.

this ghost machine senses the activity that are not visual to the human eye. A polarized glove allows its wearer to be the channel of energies roaming around freely in air and non-air. With the aid of lightwaves, outer-human existences are communicating and are stating their presence in the betweens.

humans living in craters

this is an ongoing study that explores the artificial boundaries humans have drawn between life and non-life and challenges the concept of thinking in oppositional distinctions. The project began with an extensive literature review of contemporary authors like Raymond Wiliams, Mary Douglas, Ross Exo Adams, Flemming Rafn, David Gissen, Rosi Braidotti, Donna Haraway, and others, providing insights into the norms and values upheld and reproduced through the infrastructure we are taught to live in.

drawing on an etymological study of the word ‘nature’, I engage with the critique of binary thinking in relation to gender, where society expects individuals to adhere to strict gender roles, the distinction between true and false, which dictates scientific and political debates and is closely linked to perceptions of right and wrong behaviours, and the juxtaposition of progress and stagnation, which is often used to measure social and technological progress, with anything that is not seen as progress being considered regressive or outdated. I begin to create a fictional scenario in which these boundaries become fluid. In an effort to one day turn the written story into a printed book, the project aims to present my academic writings in a more accessible way.

in another iteration, the fictional scenario has turned into a five-minute video. The video is a compilation of footage that I came across during my research. The voice-over and the subtitles are excerpts of the fiction story. Both pieces invite the audience to consider alternative ways of perceiving and structuring the world we find ourselves in.

receptor pieces

anxiety, depression, and burn-outs are topics to daily conversations in my mid-twenties social circles. I talked to friends about their tricks to feel comfortable and secure in difficult situations. During these conversations, I wrote down their secret recipes in their own words.

I transformed a large tapestry on which I had previously embroidered a personal story into a book. Using the sublimation printing technique, I printed their memories onto my story, creating a merging of experiences and sentiments between myself and my friends.

this recipe book is not intended to be be re-cooked, nor used as a manual for resolving mental health issues, but rather a tactile and sensory experience. Rifling through its soft pages aims to share loneliness’ and evoke senses of comfort and familiarities.

particularities studies

a pepper is a pepper is a pepper. Simple as that. Yet how to depict the very essence of a pepper? A whale is a whale is a whale. Yet what’s it that makes us recognize the whale?

these studies are a result of four-hour drawing sessions in which an object (the pepper) or a photo (the whale) were starting point to visual analysis. I tried to extract the very essence from my object of study. These exercises taught me to look more closely, to choose importance’s over banalities, to look at what I see without a filter.
